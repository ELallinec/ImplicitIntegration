\section{Periodic Hamiltonians and the density of states}\label{sec:framework}

This section introduces the quantum framework used throughout the paper.

\subsection{Direct and reciprocal lattices}\label{sec:lattices}

Let \(d \in \{1,2,3\}\) be the spatial dimension. A \emph{lattice} of \(\mathbb{R}^d\) is
a discrete subgroup of the form
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{R} = \left\{ A\mathbf{n} : \mathbf{n} \in \mathbb{Z}^d \right\}
    \cong \mathbb{Z}^d,
\end{equation}
where \(A = (\mathbf{a}_1\cdots\mathbf{a}_d) \in \mathbb{R}^{d\times d}\) is non-singular.
Its columns \(\mathbf{a}_i\) are the \emph{primitive vectors} of \(\mathcal{R}\). The
associated \emph{unit cell} (or Wigner-Seitz cell) is the set of points of
\(\mathbb{R}^d\) that are closer to the origin than to any other lattice site:
\begin{equation}
    \Gamma = \left\{ \mathbf{r} \in \mathbb{R}^d :
    \|\mathbf{r}\|_2 \leq \|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{R}\|_2
    \text{ for all } \mathbf{R} \in \mathcal{R}\setminus\{\mathbf{0}\}
    \right\}.
\end{equation}
By construction, \(\Gamma\) tiles \(\mathbb{R}^d\) under translation by \(\mathcal{R}\).

The \emph{reciprocal lattice} \(\mathcal{R}^*\) is the set of vectors \(\mathbf{G} \in
\mathbb{R}^d\) such that \(\mathrm{e}^{i\mathbf{G}\cdot\mathbf{R}} = 1\) for every
\(\mathbf{R} \in \mathcal{R}\). Equivalently,
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{R}^* = \left\{ B\mathbf{n} : \mathbf{n} \in \mathbb{Z}^d \right\},
    A^\intercal B = 2\pi I.
\end{equation}
The Wigner-Seitz cell of \(\mathcal{R}^*\), called the \emph{Brillouin zone}, is
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{B} = \left\{ \mathbf{k} \in \mathbb{R}^d :
    \|\mathbf{k}\|_2 \leq \|\mathbf{k}-\mathbf{G}\|_2
    \text{ for all } \mathbf{G} \in \mathcal{R}^*\setminus\{\mathbf{0}\}
    \right\}.
\end{equation}
For the standard cubic lattice \(\mathcal{R} = \mathbb{Z}^d\), \(\mathcal{B} = [-\pi,
    \pi]^d\).

\subsection{Bloch decomposition of a periodic Hamiltonian}\label{sec:bloch}

We work in a tight-binding setting: the Hamiltonian \(H\) is a self-adjoint operator on
the Hilbert space \(\mathcal{H}=\ell^2(\mathcal{R},\mathbb{C}^M)\), where \(M \geq 1\) is the number
of internal degrees of freedom (typically orbitals) attached to each lattice site. The
operator is described by a family of matrices \((H_\mathbf{R})_{\mathbf{R} \in
    \mathcal{R}}\) in \(\mathbb{C}^{M\times M}\), encoding hopping amplitudes between sites
separated by \(\mathbf{R}\), with \(H_{-\mathbf{R}} = H_\mathbf{R}^*\) (to ensure
\(H(\mathbf{k})\) is Hermitian).

Periodicity allows us to apply the \emph{Bloch transform}, which diagonalises translations
and reduces the spectral problem for \(H\) on an infinite-dimensional space to a
parametric family of finite-dimensional spectral problems indexed by \(\mathbf{k} \in
\mathcal{B}\). For each \(\mathbf{k} \in \mathcal{B}\), define
\begin{equation}
    H(\mathbf{k}) = \sum_{\mathbf{R} \in \mathcal{R}}
    \mathrm{e}^{i\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{R}} H_\mathbf{R}
    \in \mathbb{C}^{M\times M}.
    \label{eq:bloch-transform}
\end{equation}
In practice the sum is finite, so \(H(\mathbf{k})\) is a trigonometric polynomial in
\(\mathbf{k}\), hence an entire analytic function of \(\mathbf{k} \in \mathbb{C}^d\), and
is \(\mathcal{R}^*\)-periodic. We denote its eigenvalues, ordered increasingly, by
\begin{equation}
    \varepsilon_1(\mathbf{k}) \leq \varepsilon_2(\mathbf{k}) \leq \cdots
    \leq \varepsilon_M(\mathbf{k}),
\end{equation}
and refer to the maps \(\mathbf{k} \mapsto \varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k})\) as the \emph{energy
    bands}. Each \(\varepsilon_n\) is analytic and \(\mathcal{R}^*\)-periodic, but
generally only piecewise smooth: smoothness is lost at \emph{band crossings}, i.e.\ at
points \(\mathbf{k}\) where two eigenvalues coincide.

\begin{remark}
    The tight-binding setting is not restrictive in practice. Continuous Schrödinger
    operators on \(\mathbb{R}^d\) with \(\mathcal{R}\)-periodic potentials admit a similar
    Bloch decomposition. Truncating the resulting infinite spectrum to	the bands relevant
    for the energy range of interest (e.g.\ via Wannier
    interpolation~\cite{yates2007spectral}) yields a finite-dimensional model of the
    form~\eqref{eq:bloch-transform}.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Supercell discretisation and the Monkhorst-Pack grid}\label{sec:supercell}
\textcolor{red}{\textbf{Pas forcément utile dans le papier mais c'est important de
        comprendre comment les intégrales de Brillouin sont calculées en pratique.}}

For numerical purposes, the Brillouin zone is sampled on a uniform grid. This is most
easily understood through the \emph{supercell} construction. For \(L \in \mathbb{N}^*\),
the supercell is the \(L^d\)-fold replication of the unit cell along each lattice
direction:
\begin{equation}
    \Gamma_L = L \Gamma,
    \mathcal{R}_L = \mathcal{R} \cap \Gamma_L.
\end{equation}
The truncated Bloch transform of a function \(f \in L^2(\Gamma_L)\) reads
\begin{equation}
    f(\mathbf{k}) = \sum_{\mathbf{R} \in \mathcal{R}_L}
    \mathrm{e}^{i\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{R}} f(\mathbf{R}),
\end{equation}
and is evaluated on the dual of \(\Gamma_L\), namely the \emph{Monkhorst-Pack
    grid}~\cite{monkhorst1976special} (a uniform grid of \(L^d\) points in
\(\mathcal{B}\)):
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{B}_L = \left\{ \sum_{i=1}^d \frac{n_i}{L}\mathbf{b}_i :
    n_i \in \left\{-\lfloor L/2\rfloor, \dots, \lfloor L/2\rfloor - 1\right\}
    \right\},
\end{equation}
Brillouin-zone integrals are then approximated by the average
\begin{equation}
    \frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}|} \int_\mathcal{B} f(\mathbf{k})\mathrm{d}\mathbf{k}
    \approx \frac{1}{L^d} \sum_{\mathbf{k} \in \mathcal{B}_L} f(\mathbf{k}).
    \label{eq:ptr}
\end{equation}
This is the periodic trapezoidal rule on \(\mathcal{B}\) viewed as a torus. For analytic
\(\mathcal{R}^*\)-periodic integrands, it converges exponentially fast in
\(L\)~\cite{trefethen2014exponentially}.

\subsection{The density of states}\label{sec:dos}

The \emph{density of states} (DOS) of \(H\) at energy \(E \in \mathbb{R}\) is formally
defined as
\begin{equation}
    D(E) = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}|} \sum_{n=1}^M
    \int_\mathcal{B} \delta(E - \varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}))
    \mathrm{d}\mathbf{k}.
    \label{eq:dos}
\end{equation}
It is a non-negative measure on \(\mathbb{R}\), and \(D(E)\mathrm{d}E\) counts the number
of energy bands taking values in the infinitesimal interval \([E, E + \mathrm{d}E]\).

The expression~\eqref{eq:dos} is delicate to handle numerically because the integrand is a
Dirac measure. When the gradient \(\nabla \varepsilon_n\) does not vanish on
\(\{\varepsilon_n = E\}\), the co-area formula~\cite{cances2020numerical} reformulates
\(D(E)\) as a sum of surface integrals:
\begin{equation}
    D(E) = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}|} \sum_{n=1}^M
    \int_{S_n(E)} \frac{1}{|\nabla \varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k})|}
    \mathrm{d}\mathcal{H}^{d-1}(\mathbf{k}),
    \label{eq:dosgrad}
\end{equation}
where \(\mathcal{H}^{d-1}\) is the \((d-1)\)-dimensional Hausdorff measure and
\begin{equation}
    S_n(E) = \{\mathbf{k} \in \mathcal{B} : \varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}) = E\}
\end{equation}
is the \(n\)-th \emph{Fermi surface} at energy \(E\). The energies at which the
non-vanishing-gradient condition fails (or at which two bands cross at energy \(E\)) are
called \emph{van Hove singularities}; they form a set of measure zero and are responsible
for the only points where \(D\) is singular~\cite{vanhove1953occurrence}. In 1D the DOS
diverges as \(|E - \varepsilon_n(k_0)|^{-1/2}\) at a van Hove singularity \(k_0\), while
in 2D it typically diverges as \(|\log|E - \varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}_0)||\) at an isolated
singularity \(\mathbf{k}_0\) and as \(|E - \varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}_0)|^{-1/2}\) along a
singular curve of points \(\mathbf{k}_0\). In 3D, the DOS is typically continuous at van
Hove singularities, but exhibits cusp-like features.

Equation~\eqref{eq:dosgrad} is the starting point of our numerical method: rather than
regularising the Dirac in~\eqref{eq:dos}, we discretise the right-hand side directly,
using a high-order quadrature on implicitly defined surfaces.

\section{High-order quadrature on implicitly defined surfaces}\label{sec:saye}

We now recall the high-order quadrature method of Saye~\cite{saye2015highorder} for the
integration of smooth functions over surfaces and volumes defined implicitly inside a
hyperrectangle. Specialising it to \(\phi = \varepsilon_n - E\) will yield a quadrature
for the DOS via~\eqref{eq:dosgrad}.

\subsection{Setting}

Let \(U = (x_1^L, x_1^U) \times \cdots \times (x_d^L, x_d^U) \subset \mathbb{R}^d\) be a
hyperrectangle and \(\phi \in \mathcal{C}^k(U)\) a level-set function (with \(k\) at least
equal to the desired order of accuracy). We seek to evaluate
\begin{equation}
    \int_{\Gamma \cap U} g \mathrm{d}\mathcal{H}^{d-1},
    \Gamma = \{\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^d : \phi(\mathbf{x}) = 0\},
\end{equation}
for a smooth integrand \(g\). We denote by \((\mathbf{e}_i)_{i=1}^d\) the canonical basis
of \(\mathbb{R}^d\).

\subsection{Dimension reduction via the implicit function theorem}

The key idea is to express \(\Gamma \cap U\) locally as the graph of an implicit
\emph{height function} over a \((d-1)\)-dimensional sub-hyperrectangle, thereby reducing
the integration on a \((d-1)\)-dimensional surface in \(\mathbb{R}^d\) to an integration
on a \((d-1)\)-dimensional volume in \(\mathbb{R}^{d-1}\).

Suppose we can find a coordinate direction \(i \in \{1,\dots,d\}\) along which \(\phi\) is
monotone on \(U\), in the sense that \(\partial_i \phi\) is bounded away from zero on
\(U\). By the implicit function theorem, the equation \(\phi(\mathbf{x}) = 0\) then
defines a unique smooth height function \(h : \tilde{U} \to (x_i^L, x_i^U)\), where
\(\tilde U \subset \mathbb{R}^{d-1}\) is the projection of \(U\) onto the coordinates
\(\tilde{\mathbf{x}} = (x_1, \dots, x_{i-1}, x_{i+1}, \dots, x_d)\), satisfying
\begin{equation}
    \phi\left(\tilde{\mathbf{x}} + h(\tilde{\mathbf{x}})\mathbf{e}_i\right) = 0,
    \tilde{\mathbf{x}} \in \tilde U.
    \label{eq:height}
\end{equation}
The subdomain \(\tilde V \subset \tilde U\) on which the graph \(\tilde{\mathbf{x}}
\mapsto \tilde{\mathbf{x}} + h(\tilde{\mathbf{x}})\mathbf{e}_i\) indeed lies in \(U\) is
determined by the signs of the restrictions of \(\phi\) to the lower and upper faces of
\(U\) in the direction \(\mathbf{e}_i\):
\begin{equation}
    \psi^L(\tilde{\mathbf{x}}) = \phi(\tilde{\mathbf{x}} + x_i^L \mathbf{e}_i),
    \psi^U(\tilde{\mathbf{x}}) = \phi(\tilde{\mathbf{x}} + x_i^U \mathbf{e}_i).
\end{equation}
For instance, when \(\partial_i \phi > 0\) on \(U\), one has \(\tilde V = \{\psi^L < 0\}
\cap \{\psi^U > 0\}\). The other case is symmetric. All configurations are illustrated
in~\autoref{fig:delimitation}.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[scale=0.45]{Images/Hyperrectangle.png}
    \caption{Cases for the definition of \(V_1\) and \(V_2\) (image
        from~\cite{saye2015highorder}).} \label{fig:delimitation} % chktex 24
\end{figure}
A short computation using~\eqref{eq:height} gives the area element on \(\Gamma\):
\begin{equation}
    \sqrt{1 + \|\nabla_{\tilde{\mathbf{x}}} h\|^2}
    = \left.\frac{|\nabla \phi|}{|\partial_i \phi|}\right|_{\mathbf{x} = \tilde{\mathbf{x}} + h(\tilde{\mathbf{x}})\mathbf{e}_i},
\end{equation}
so that
\begin{equation}
    \int_{\Gamma \cap U} g \mathrm{d}\mathcal{H}^{d-1}
    = \int_{\tilde V}
    g\left(\tilde{\mathbf{x}} + h(\tilde{\mathbf{x}})\mathbf{e}_i\right)
    \left.\frac{|\nabla \phi|}{|\partial_i \phi|}\right|_{\mathbf{x} = \tilde{\mathbf{x}} + h(\tilde{\mathbf{x}})\mathbf{e}_i}
    \mathrm{d}\tilde{\mathbf{x}}.
    \label{eq:saye-reduction}
\end{equation}
The \((d-1)\)-dimensional integral on the right-hand side can itself be reduced to a
\((d-2)\)-dimensional one by applying the same procedure recursively, until a
one-dimensional quadrature is reached. The final scheme is a tensor-product Gauss-Legendre
quadrature in which the height function \(h\) is evaluated by a one-dimensional
root-finding procedure for \(\phi\).

\subsection{Validity and the role of guaranteed bounds}\label{sec:saye-bounds}

For the dimension-reduction step~\eqref{eq:saye-reduction} to be valid, two ingredients
must be checked on \(U\):
\begin{enumerate}
    \item there exists a coordinate direction \(i\) along which \(\partial_i \phi\) has
          constant, known sign on \(U\);
    \item the signs of \(\psi^L\) and \(\psi^U\) on \(\tilde U\) are correctly identified,
          so that \(\tilde V\) matches the true projection of \(\Gamma \cap U\).
\end{enumerate}
Both checks reduce to verifying inequalities of the form ``\(\phi\) (or \(\partial_i
\phi\)) does not change sign on a hyperrectangle''. Saye's algorithm performs them by
computing \emph{guaranteed bounds} on \(\phi\) and \(\partial_i \phi\) over \(U\). If a
sign cannot be ascertained on \(U\), the box is bisected and the procedure recurses on the
sub-boxes. The overall convergence therefore hinges entirely on the quality of the
available bounds on \(\phi\) and its first partial derivatives.

\subsection{Application to the density of states}

For the DOS, we apply this procedure to each band \(n\) and to the level-set function
\begin{equation}
    \phi_{n, E}(\mathbf{k}) = \varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}) - E.
\end{equation}
Combined with~\eqref{eq:dosgrad}, this yields a quadrature for \(D(E)\) as a sum, over a
hierarchical subdivision of \(\mathcal{B}\), of integrals of \(g = 1 / |\nabla
\varepsilon_n|\) over the local pieces of \(S_n(E)\).

The boxes produced by the subdivision are centered hyperrectangles
\begin{equation}
    D = \mathbf{k}_0 + \bm{\delta\mathbf{k}} =
    \{\mathbf{k} \in \mathcal{B} : |k_i - k_{0,i}| \leq \delta k_i,\
    i = 1, \dots, d\},
\end{equation}
and the bounds required by Saye's algorithm are bounds on \(\varepsilon_n - E\),
\(\partial_i \varepsilon_n\) and (for the integrand \(1/|\nabla \varepsilon_n|\)) on the
second derivatives \(\partial_i \partial_j \varepsilon_n\), uniformly on each such box
\(D\).

\section{Theoretical preliminaries}\label{sec:preliminaries}

We collect in this section the analytic tools needed to construct,
in~\autoref{sec:bounds}, computable bounds on the Bloch eigenvalues and their derivatives.

Throughout, \(H : \mathcal{B} \to \mathbb{C}^{M\times M}\) denotes an analytic Bloch
Hamiltonian at som generic point \(\mathbf{k}\) of \(\mathcal{B}\).  \(\varepsilon_n\), \(\psi_n\)
denote its eigenvalues and normalised eigenvectors at the same point \(\mathbf{k}\).

\subsection{Matrix norms}\label{sec:matrix-norms}

We use two matrix norms on \(\mathbb{C}^{M\times M}\). The \emph{operator (spectral) norm}
is the induced norm associated with the Euclidean norm on \(\mathbb{C}^M\):
\begin{equation}
    \|A\|_\mathrm{op} = \sigma_\mathrm{max}(A)
    = \sqrt{\lambda_\mathrm{max}(A^*A)}.
\end{equation}
The \emph{Frobenius norm} is
\begin{equation}
    \|A\|_F = \sqrt{\sum_{i,j=1}^M |A_{ij}|^2}
    = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^M \sigma_i(A)^2}.
\end{equation}
For Hermitian \(H\), the spectral norm coincides with the spectral radius:
\begin{equation}
    \|H\|_\mathrm{op} = \max_{\|x\|_2 = 1} \langle x | H | x \rangle
    = \max_{1 \leq i \leq M} |\lambda_i(H)|.
\end{equation}
The two norms are related by the elementary inequality
\begin{equation}
    \|A\|_\mathrm{op} \leq \|A\|_F \leq \sqrt{M}\|A\|_\mathrm{op}.
    \label{eq:op-vs-frob}
\end{equation}

\begin{remark}
    For Hermitian matrices, the induced \(1\)-norm and \(\infty\)-norm coincide, but they
    are not unitarily invariant and are therefore ill-suited for spectral perturbation
    results; we do not use them in this paper. The spectral norm is natural for eigenvalue
    perturbation estimates, yet it is numerically difficult to bound over a set of
    parameters without solving a global optimization problem. The Frobenius norm, in
    contrast, is easily computable from the matrix entries and can be used to bound
    parameter-dependent spectral norms via~\eqref{eq:op-vs-frob}, albeit at the cost of a
    factor \(\sqrt{M}\).
\end{remark}


\subsection{Weyl's inequality}\label{sec:weyl}

The starting point of all our eigenvalue bounds is Weyl's inequality, which controls the
perturbation of individual eigenvalues by the spectral norm of the perturbation. We state
it directly in the form best suited to the \(\mathbf{k}\)-dependence of the Bloch
Hamiltonian.

\begin{lemma}[Weyl's inequality for Bloch eigenvalues]
    \label{lemma:weyl} % chktex 24
    For all \(n \in \{1,\dots,M\}\) and all \(\mathbf{k}, \mathbf{k}_0 \in \mathcal{B}\),
    \begin{equation}
        |\varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}) - \varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}_0)|
        \leq \|H(\mathbf{k}) - H(\mathbf{k}_0)\|_\mathrm{op}.
    \end{equation}
\end{lemma}

This inequality holds without any non-degeneracy assumption and relies only on the
continuity of \(H\). Its strength is to reduce the analysis of the (generally non-smooth)
eigenvalue maps \(\varepsilon_n\) to the analysis of the (entire and analytic)
matrix-valued \(H\).

\subsection{Time-independent perturbation theory: non-degenerate
    case}\label{sec:perturbation-nondegen}

Weyl's inequality controls eigenvalue \emph{values} but says nothing about their
\emph{derivatives}, which we need to control the integrand \(1/|\nabla \varepsilon_n|\)
of~\eqref{eq:dosgrad} and to certify the applicability of the implicit function theorem in
Saye's algorithm. To access derivatives, we use standard time-independent perturbation
theory, which expands the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of a perturbed self-adjoint
operator in powers of the perturbation parameter.

\subsubsection{General setup}

Consider three Hermitian matrices \(H_0, \delta H, H \in \mathbb{C}^{M\times M}\) related
by
\begin{equation}
    H(\lambda) = H_0 + \lambda\delta H,
\end{equation}
where \(\lambda \in \mathbb{R}\) is a small parameter. Let \(\varepsilon_{n,0},
\psi_{n,0}\) denote the eigenpairs of \(H_0\), assumed non-degenerate, and let
\(\varepsilon_n(\lambda), \psi_n(\lambda)\) denote those of \(H(\lambda)\), depending
analytically on \(\lambda\) for \(\lambda\) sufficiently small. Expanding
\begin{equation}
    \varepsilon_n(\lambda) = \sum_{i=0}^{+\infty} \lambda^i\varepsilon_n^{(i)}
\end{equation}
and identifying coefficients term by term, we obtain up to second
order~\cite{kato1995perturbation},
\begin{equation}
    \varepsilon_n(\lambda)
    = \varepsilon_{n,0}
    + \lambda\langle \psi_{n,0} | \delta H | \psi_{n,0} \rangle
    + \lambda^2 \sum_{m \neq n}
    \frac{|\langle \psi_{m,0} | \delta H | \psi_{n,0} \rangle|^2}
    {\varepsilon_{n,0} - \varepsilon_{m,0}}
    + \mathcal{O}(\lambda^3).
    \label{eq:rsbg}
\end{equation}
The first-order coefficient is the projection of \(\delta H\) onto the eigenstate
\(\psi_{n,0}\); the second-order coefficient involves all other eigenstates and is
inversely proportional to the energy gaps separating \(\varepsilon_{n,0}\) from the rest
of the spectrum.

\subsubsection{Application to the Bloch Hamiltonian}

We now specialise this expansion to perturbations in \(\mathbf{k}\). Fix \(\mathbf{k}_0
\in \mathcal{B}\) and consider the Taylor expansion (as \(H\) is analytic)
\begin{equation}
    H(\mathbf{k}) = H(\mathbf{k}_0)
    + \sum_{i=1}^d \delta k_i \partial_i H(\mathbf{k}_0)
    + \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i,j=1}^d \delta k_i \delta k_j
    \partial_i\partial_j H(\mathbf{k}_0)
    + \mathcal{O}(\|\bm{\delta\mathbf{k}}\|^3),
\end{equation}
with \(\delta k_i = k_i - k_{0,i}\). Plugging this into~\eqref{eq:rsbg}, one recovers the
standard expressions for the Taylor expansion of \(\varepsilon_n\).
\begin{equation}
    \varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}) = \varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}_0)
    + \sum_{i=1}^d \delta k_i \partial_i \varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}_0)
    + \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i,j=1}^d \delta k_i \delta k_j
    \partial_i\partial_j \varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}_0)
    + \mathcal{O}(\|\bm{\delta\mathbf{k}}\|^3),
\end{equation}
Writing
\(\psi_n = \psi_n(\mathbf{k}_0)\) for brevity, the first derivative is given by the
Hellmann-Feynman formula
\begin{equation}
    \partial_i \varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}_0)
    = \langle \psi_n | \partial_i H(\mathbf{k}_0) | \psi_n \rangle,
    \label{eq:hellmann-feynman}
\end{equation}
and the second derivative is
\begin{equation}
    \partial_i \partial_j \varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}_0)
    = \langle \psi_n | \partial_i \partial_j H(\mathbf{k}_0) | \psi_n \rangle
    + 2 \sum_{m \neq n}
    \frac{\mathrm{Re}\left(\langle \psi_n | \partial_i H(\mathbf{k}_0) | \psi_m \rangle
        \langle \psi_m | \partial_j H(\mathbf{k}_0) | \psi_n \rangle\right)}
    {\varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}_0) - \varepsilon_m(\mathbf{k}_0)}.
    \label{eq:second-derivative}
\end{equation}
The denominator \(\varepsilon_n - \varepsilon_m\) in~\eqref{eq:second-derivative} encodes
the influence of the rest of the spectrum on the curvature of the band \(\varepsilon_n\).
It also indicates the regime in which the expansion fails: when another band approaches
\(\varepsilon_n\), the second derivative diverges and the eigenvalue ceases to be smooth.


\section{Bounds for eigenvalues and derivatives}\label{sec:bounds}

We now combine the analytic tools of~\autoref{sec:preliminaries} into computable
bounds on the Bloch eigenvalues and their derivatives. All bounds are established
on a fixed centered box
\begin{equation}
    D = \mathbf{k}_0 + \bm{\delta\mathbf{k}}
    = \left\{ \mathbf{k} \in \mathcal{B} : |k_i - k_{0,i}| \leq \delta k_i,\
    i = 1, \dots, d \right\},
    \label{eq:box}
\end{equation}
issued from the adaptive subdivision performed by Saye's algorithm. Throughout,
the half-widths \(\delta k_i \geq 0\) are non-negative scalars. We adopt the
convention that the path bounds are first stated in operator norm — the natural
norm for spectral perturbation arguments — and only later substituted by
Frobenius norms for numerical evaluation (see~\autoref{sec:numerical-bounds}).

\subsection{The path bound}\label{sec:bounds-pathwise}

The starting point of all our eigenvalue bounds is the following pathwise estimate,
which controls the variation of the Hamiltonian on \(D\) by the first derivatives
of \(H\). The result is stated as a chain of two inequalities of decreasing
sharpness, corresponding to two ways of treating the linear combination
\(\sum_j \partial_j H(\mathbf{q})\,(k_j - k_{0,j})\): either keeping the matrix
absolute value as a single object (sharper, but requires a spectral decomposition
to evaluate) or splitting it into individual operator norms (looser, but
amenable to a Frobenius substitution).

\begin{lemma}[First-order path bound]\label{lemma:bounds}
    For all \(\mathbf{k} \in D\),
    \begin{align}
        \|H(\mathbf{k}) - H(\mathbf{k}_0)\|_\mathrm{op}
         & \leq \sup_{\mathbf{q}\in D}
        \left\| \sum_{j=1}^d |\partial_j H(\mathbf{q})|\, \delta k_j
        \right\|_\mathrm{op}
        \label{eq:pathwise-tight}       \\
         & \leq \sup_{\mathbf{q} \in D}
        \sum_{j=1}^d \|\partial_j H(\mathbf{q})\|_\mathrm{op}\, \delta k_j.
        \label{eq:pathwise-loose}
    \end{align}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
    The proof proceeds in two steps, one per inequality.

    \emph{Step 1 (path bound,~\eqref{eq:pathwise-tight}).}
    Since \(D\) is convex (it's a rectangle), the segment
    \(\gamma : t \in [0,1] \mapsto (1-t)\mathbf{k}_0 + t\mathbf{k}\) lies in \(D\).
    The map \(t \mapsto H(\gamma(t))\) is analytic, and the chain rule gives
    \(\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t} H(\gamma(t))
    = \sum_{j=1}^d \partial_j H(\gamma(t))\,(k_j - k_{0,j})\). Integrating from
    \(0\) to \(1\),
    \begin{equation}
        H(\mathbf{k}) - H(\mathbf{k}_0)
        = \int_0^1 \sum_{j=1}^d \partial_j H(\gamma(t))\,(k_j - k_{0,j})
        \,\mathrm{d}t.
        \label{eq:H-integral-form}
    \end{equation}
    Fix \(t \in [0,1]\) and set \(B(t) := \sum_j \partial_j H(\gamma(t))\,
    (k_j - k_{0,j})\). The matrix \(B(t)\) is Hermitian as a real linear
    combination of Hermitian matrices. We now establish the pointwise inequality
    \begin{equation}
        \|B(t)\|_\mathrm{op}
        \leq \left\| \sum_{j=1}^d |\partial_j H(\gamma(t))|\, |k_j - k_{0,j}|
        \right\|_\mathrm{op},
        \label{eq:pointwise-jkn}
    \end{equation}
    which is the heart of the argument. For any Hermitian matrix \(A\) with
    spectral decomposition \(A = \sum_i \mu_i P_i\) and any unit vector \(v\),
    \begin{equation}
        |\langle v, A v\rangle|
        = \left| \sum_i \mu_i\,\|P_i v\|^2 \right|
        \leq \sum_i |\mu_i|\,\|P_i v\|^2
        = \langle v, |A|\, v\rangle,
        \label{eq:spectral-ineq}
    \end{equation}
    Combining~\eqref{eq:spectral-ineq} with the variational principle
    \(\|B(t)\|_\mathrm{op} = \max_{\|v\|=1} |\langle v, B(t) v\rangle|\)
    (valid for Hermitian \(B(t)\)),
    \begin{align}
        \|B(t)\|_\mathrm{op}
         & = \max_{\|v\|=1}
        \left| \sum_j (k_j - k_{0,j})\, \langle v, \partial_j H(\gamma(t))\, v\rangle
        \right|                                                                  \\
         & \leq \max_{\|v\|=1}
        \sum_j |k_j - k_{0,j}|\, |\langle v, \partial_j H(\gamma(t))\, v\rangle| \\
         & \leq \max_{\|v\|=1}
        \sum_j |k_j - k_{0,j}|\, \langle v, |\partial_j H(\gamma(t))|\, v\rangle \\
         & = \left\| \sum_j |\partial_j H(\gamma(t))|\, |k_j - k_{0,j}|
        \right\|_\mathrm{op},
    \end{align}
    where the last equality uses that \(\sum_j |\partial_j H(\gamma(t))|\, |k_j -
    k_{0,j}|\) is positive semi-definite (a non-negative combination of PSD matrices), so
    its operator norm coincides with its largest eigenvalue. This
    proves~\eqref{eq:pointwise-jkn}.

    Taking the operator norm of~\eqref{eq:H-integral-form} via the Bochner
    integral triangle inequality and applying~\eqref{eq:pointwise-jkn},
    \begin{equation}
        \|H(\mathbf{k}) - H(\mathbf{k}_0)\|_\mathrm{op}
        \leq \int_0^1 \|B(t)\|_\mathrm{op}\,\mathrm{d}t
        \leq \int_0^1 \left\| \sum_j |\partial_j H(\gamma(t))|\, |k_j - k_{0,j}|
        \right\|_\mathrm{op}\,\mathrm{d}t.
        \label{eq:integrated-jkn}
    \end{equation}
    To replace the path-dependent integrand by a uniform bound on \(D\), we use
    \(|k_j - k_{0,j}| \leq \delta k_j\) and the monotonicity of the operator
    norm for positive semi-deff positive seminite matrices: if \(0 \leq P
    \leq Q\) (in the PSD sense) then \(\|P\|_\mathrm{op}\leq\|Q\|_\mathrm{op}\).
    Applied to
    \begin{equation}
        \sum_j |\partial_j H(\gamma(t))|\, |k_j - k_{0,j}|
        \leq \sum_j |\partial_j H(\gamma(t))|\, \delta k_j,
    \end{equation}
    this gives
    \begin{equation}
        \left\| \sum_j |\partial_j H(\gamma(t))|\, |k_j - k_{0,j}|
        \right\|_\mathrm{op}
        \leq \left\| \sum_j |\partial_j H(\gamma(t))|\, \delta k_j
        \right\|_\mathrm{op}
        \leq \sup_{\mathbf{q} \in D}
        \left\| \sum_j |\partial_j H(\mathbf{q})|\, \delta k_j
        \right\|_\mathrm{op},
    \end{equation}
    where the last inequality uses \(\gamma(t) \in D\). The right-hand side is
    constant in \(t\), so integrating over \([0,1]\)
    yields~\eqref{eq:pathwise-tight}.

    Since \(\||\partial_j H(\mathbf{q})|\|_\mathrm{op} = \|\partial_j
    H(\mathbf{q})\|_\mathrm{op}\) (the operator norm is invariant under taking the matrix
    absolute value, as it depends only on singular values), applying the triangular
    inequality yields~\eqref{eq:pathwise-loose}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Sharpness: \eqref{eq:pathwise-tight} versus \eqref{eq:pathwise-loose}]
    The bound~\eqref{eq:pathwise-tight} can be strictly tighter than
    ~\eqref{eq:pathwise-loose}, with the gap controlled by the spectral
    overlap of the matrices \((|\partial_j H(\mathbf{q})|)_j\). Two extreme
    cases illustrate the spread.
    \begin{itemize}
        \item If all \(|\partial_j H(\mathbf{q})|\) share an eigenvector
              \(v\) achieving their largest eigenvalue, then equality holds:
              \(\|\sum_j |\partial_j H(\mathbf{q})|\,\delta k_j\|_\mathrm{op}
              = \sum_j \|\partial_j H(\mathbf{q})\|_\mathrm{op}\,\delta k_j\).
        \item If the \(|\partial_j H(\mathbf{q})|\) are mutually orthogonal
              projectors (e.g.\ \(|A_1| = |e_1\rangle\langle e_1|\),
              \(|A_2| = |e_2\rangle\langle e_2|\)), then
              \(\|\sum_j |\partial_j H|\,\delta k_j\|_\mathrm{op}
              = \max_j \delta k_j\), whereas
              \(\sum_j \|\partial_j H\|_\mathrm{op}\,\delta k_j
              = \sum_j \delta k_j\), so the second bound is up to \(d\) times
              looser.
    \end{itemize}
    A naive Cauchy-Schwarz bound \(|\langle v, \partial_j H\, v\rangle|
    \leq \|\partial_j H\|_\mathrm{op}\) directly applied to
    \(\|\sum_j (k_j - k_{0,j}) \partial_j H\|_\mathrm{op}\) gives the looser
    bound~\eqref{eq:pathwise-loose} only; the spectral
    inequality~\eqref{eq:spectral-ineq} is what allows~\eqref{eq:pathwise-tight}.
\end{remark}
\begin{remark}[Matrix absolute value and spectral decomposition]
    The matrix absolute value \(|A|\) of a Hermitian matrix \(A\) is defined as \(|A| =
    \sqrt{A^2}\). It can be computed from the spectral decomposition of \(A\): if \(A =
    \sum_i \mu_i P_i\) then \(|A| = \sum_i |\mu_i| P_i\). The bound using \(|\partial_j
    H|\) is therefore sharper than the one using \(\|\partial_j H\|_\mathrm{op}\) because
    it retains the spectral information of \(\partial_j H\) instead of discarding it via
    the operator norm. However, it is not computable on an arbitrary set \(D\) without
    solving a global optimization problem, since the spectral decomposition of \(\sum_j
    |\partial_j H(\mathbf{q})|\,\delta k_j\) depends on \(\mathbf{q}\). The bound using
    \(\|\partial_j H\|_\mathrm{op}\) is therefore more tractable.
\end{remark}
\subsection{First-order eigenvalue bounds}\label{sec:bounds-eigenvalues-1}

Combining Weyl's inequality (\autoref{lemma:weyl}) with the path
bound~\eqref{eq:pathwise-tight}–\eqref{eq:pathwise-loose} yields the order-one
bound on the eigenvalues, valid uniformly in \(n\) and without any non-degeneracy
assumption.

\begin{corollary}[First-order eigenvalue bound]\label{cor:1st-eigenvalue-bound}
    For all \(n \in \{1,\dots,M\}\) and all \(\mathbf{k} \in D\),
    \begin{align}
        |\varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}) - \varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}_0)|
         & \leq \sup_{\mathbf{q}\in D}
        \left\| \sum_{j=1}^d |\partial_j H(\mathbf{q})|\, \delta k_j
        \right\|_\mathrm{op}
        \label{eq:eig-bound-tight}      \\
         & \leq \sup_{\mathbf{q} \in D}
        \sum_{j=1}^d \|\partial_j H(\mathbf{q})\|_\mathrm{op}\, \delta k_j.
        \label{eq:eig-bound-loose}
    \end{align}
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
    Direct application of~\autoref{lemma:weyl} and~\autoref{lemma:bounds}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Range of validity]
    Both bounds are valid uniformly in \(n\) and require no assumption on the
    multiplicity or isolation of \(\varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}_0)\), since Weyl's
    inequality and the path bound both hold for every hermitian Hamiltonian.
    They are therefore the appropriate bounds for boxes that may contain band
    crossings, where the order-two bound below fails.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Second-order eigenvalue bound}\label{sec:bounds-eigenvalues-2}

A sharper bound is obtained by pushing the Taylor expansion of \(\varepsilon_n\)
to second order, at the price of requiring a spectral gap.

\begin{lemma}[Second-derivative bound]\label{lemma:second-deriv}
    Assume that the band \(\varepsilon_n\) is non-degenerate on \(D\), i.e.\
    \(\Delta_n(D) > 0\), with
    \begin{equation}
        \Delta_n(D) := \inf_{\mathbf{k} \in D}\,\min_{m \neq n}
        |\varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}) - \varepsilon_m(\mathbf{k})|.
    \end{equation}
    Then for all \(\mathbf{q} \in D\) and all \(i, j \in \{1, \dots, d\}\),
    \begin{equation}
        |\partial_i \partial_j \varepsilon_n(\mathbf{q})|
        \leq \mu_{ij}(\mathbf{q})
        := \|\partial_i \partial_j H(\mathbf{q})\|_\mathrm{op}
        + \frac{2 \|\partial_i H(\mathbf{q})\|_\mathrm{op}\,
            \|\partial_j H(\mathbf{q})\|_\mathrm{op}}
        {\Delta_n(D)}.
        \label{eq:mu-def}
    \end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
    Under the non-degeneracy assumption, \(\varepsilon_n\) is real-analytic
    on \(D\), and the second-derivative formula~\eqref{eq:second-derivative}
    applies. The triangle inequality decomposes \(|\partial_i \partial_j
    \varepsilon_n(\mathbf{q})|\) into two terms.

    The first term is bounded as
    \begin{equation}
        |\langle \psi_n | \partial_i \partial_j H(\mathbf{q}) | \psi_n \rangle|
        \leq \|\partial_i \partial_j H(\mathbf{q})\|_\mathrm{op},
    \end{equation}
    since \(\|\psi_n\|_2 = 1\).

    For the second term, the gap denominator is bounded below by
    \(\Delta_n(D)\), and the numerator is controlled via the
    Cauchy–Schwarz inequality:
    \begin{align}
        \left| \sum_{m \neq n}
        \langle \psi_n | A | \psi_m \rangle \langle \psi_m | B | \psi_n \rangle
        \right|
         & \leq \sum_{m \neq n}
        |\langle \psi_n | A | \psi_m \rangle|\,|\langle \psi_m | B | \psi_n \rangle| \\
         & \leq \left(\sum_{m \neq n}
                |\langle \psi_n | A | \psi_m \rangle|^2\right)^{1/2}
        \left(\sum_{m \neq n}
        |\langle \psi_m | B | \psi_n \rangle|^2\right)^{1/2}                         \\
         & \leq \|A \psi_n\|_2\,\|B^* \psi_n\|_2
        \leq \|A\|_\mathrm{op}\,\|B\|_\mathrm{op},
    \end{align}
    where we used that \(\sum_{m \neq n} |\langle \psi_n | A | \psi_m \rangle|^2
    \leq \sum_m |\langle \psi_n | A | \psi_m \rangle|^2 = \|A\psi_n\|_2^2\).
    Setting \(A = \partial_i H(\mathbf{q})\) and
    \(B = \partial_j H(\mathbf{q})\) yields the second term in~\eqref{eq:mu-def}, and the result follows.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Second-order eigenvalue bound]\label{cor:2nd-eigenvalue-bound}
    Under the assumptions of~\autoref{lemma:second-deriv}, for all
    \(\mathbf{k} \in D\),
    \begin{equation}
        \left| \varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}) - \varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}_0)
        - \sum_{i=1}^d \partial_i \varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}_0)\,(k_i - k_{0,i})
        \right|
        \leq \frac{1}{2} \sup_{\mathbf{q} \in D}
        \sum_{i,j=1}^d \mu_{ij}(\mathbf{q})\, \delta k_i\, \delta k_j.
        \label{eq:eig-bound-order-2}
    \end{equation}
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
    The non-degeneracy assumption guarantees that \(\varepsilon_n\) is
    \(\mathcal{C}^2\) on \(D\). By Taylor--Lagrange applied to
    there exists \(\mathbf{q}\) on the segment
    \([\mathbf{k}_0, \mathbf{k}] \subset D\) such that
    \begin{equation}
        \varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}) - \varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}_0)
        - \sum_i \partial_i \varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}_0)\,(k_i - k_{0,i})
        = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i,j}
        \partial_i \partial_j \varepsilon_n(\mathbf{q})\,(k_i - k_{0,i})(k_j - k_{0,j}).
    \end{equation}
    Bounding each \(|\partial_i \partial_j \varepsilon_n(\mathbf{q})|\) by
    \(\mu_{ij}(\mathbf{q})\) via~\autoref{lemma:second-deriv} and using
    \(|k_i - k_{0,i}| \leq \delta k_i\) together with \(\mathbf{q} \in D\) yields the
    claim.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[When does the order-two bound improve over the order-one bound?]
    Unlike~\autoref{cor:1st-eigenvalue-bound}, the order-two bound is not uniform
    in \(n\): it depends on the gap \(\Delta_n(D)\), and is therefore meaningful
    only on boxes where the band \(\varepsilon_n\) is isolated and the gap is
    not too small. Asymptotically as \(\bm{\delta\mathbf{k}} \to 0\) at fixed
    gap, the right-hand side of~\eqref{eq:eig-bound-order-2} is
    \(\mathcal{O}(\|\bm{\delta\mathbf{k}}\|^2)\), whereas the right-hand side of
    \eqref{eq:eig-bound-tight} controls \(\varepsilon_n - \varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}_0)\)
    at order \(\mathcal{O}(\|\bm{\delta\mathbf{k}}\|)\). The order-two bound is
    therefore preferable on small enough boxes; the crossover scale is roughly
    \(\|\bm{\delta\mathbf{k}}\| \sim \Delta_n(D) /
    \|\partial_i H\|_\mathrm{op}\), the inverse of the Hamiltonian Lipschitz
    constant relative to the gap.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Bounds on the spectral gap}\label{sec:bounds-gap}

The order-two bound and the derivative bound below involve the inverse of the gap
\(1/\Delta_n(D)\) throughs second order terms. To make those bounds effective, we need a
guaranteed strictly positive lower bound on \(\Delta_n(D)\). Two such bounds follow as
immediate corollaries of the eigenvalue bounds, and a sharper one is obtained from the
order-two bound.

\begin{corollary}[Lower bounds on the spectral gap]\label{cor:gap-bounds}
    For any \(\mathbf{k} \in D\) and any \(m \neq n\),
    \begin{equation}
        |\varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}) - \varepsilon_m(\mathbf{k})|
        \geq |\varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}_0) - \varepsilon_m(\mathbf{k}_0)|
        - |\varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}) - \varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}_0)|
        - |\varepsilon_m(\mathbf{k}) - \varepsilon_m(\mathbf{k}_0)|.
        \label{eq:gap-triangle}
    \end{equation}
    Bounding the last two terms by~\autoref{lemma:weyl} or by the path
    bound~\eqref{eq:pathwise-loose} yields, respectively,
    \begin{align}
        \Delta_n(D)
         & \geq \min_{m \neq n}
        \left( |\varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}_0) - \varepsilon_m(\mathbf{k}_0)|
        - 2 \sup_{\mathbf{q} \in D}
        \|H(\mathbf{q}) - H(\mathbf{k}_0)\|_\mathrm{op} \right)_+,
        \label{eq:gap-order-0}  \\[4pt]
        \Delta_n(D)
         & \geq \min_{m \neq n}
        \left( |\varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}_0) - \varepsilon_m(\mathbf{k}_0)|
        - 2 \sup_{\mathbf{q} \in D}
        \sum_{j=1}^d \|\partial_j H(\mathbf{q})\|_\mathrm{op}\,
        \delta k_j \right)_+,
        \label{eq:gap-order-1}
    \end{align}
    where \((x)_+ = \max(x, 0)\). The same inequalities hold with
    \(\|\cdot\|_\mathrm{op}\) replaced by the tighter
    bound~\eqref{eq:pathwise-tight}.
\end{corollary}

\begin{remark}
    Both bounds are informative only when their right-hand side is strictly positive, that
    is, when the band \(\varepsilon_n\) remains isolated from its neighbours on \(D\). The
    non-positivity of~\eqref{eq:gap-order-1} can signal near-degeneracies, and trigger a
    subdivision of \(D\) by Saye's algorithm.
\end{remark}

A still sharper gap bound can be derived from the order-two eigenvalue
bound~\eqref{eq:eig-bound-order-2}. Unlike the previous two bounds, this one is
no longer uniform in \(n\): the band of interest \(\varepsilon_n\) and a
competing band \(\varepsilon_m\) generally have different remainders.

\begin{corollary}[Order-two lower bound on the spectral gap]\label{cor:gap-order-2}
    Writing \(L_n(\mathbf{k}) := \sum_i \partial_i \varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}_0)\,
    (k_i - k_{0,i})\) for the linear part and
    \(Q_n(D) := \tfrac{1}{2} \sup_{\mathbf{q} \in D}
    \sum_{i,j} \mu_{ij}(\mathbf{q})\, \delta k_i\, \delta k_j\) for the quadratic
    remainder of band \(n\), with \(\mu_{ij}\) defined in~\eqref{eq:mu-def},
    \begin{equation}
        \Delta_n(D) \geq \min_{m \neq n}\,\inf_{\mathbf{k} \in D}
        \left( |\varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}_0) - \varepsilon_m(\mathbf{k}_0)
        + L_n(\mathbf{k}) - L_m(\mathbf{k})|
        - Q_n(D) - Q_m(D) \right)_+.
        \label{eq:gap-order-2}
    \end{equation}
    The inner infimum of \(L_n - L_m\) over \(\mathbf{k} \in D\) is explicit:
    being affine in \(\mathbf{k}\), it is attained at a vertex of \(D\).
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
    Apply~\autoref{cor:2nd-eigenvalue-bound} to bands \(n\) and \(m\):
    \(\varepsilon_l(\mathbf{k}) = \varepsilon_l(\mathbf{k}_0) + L_l(\mathbf{k})
    + r_l(\mathbf{k})\) with \(|r_l(\mathbf{k})| \leq R_l(D)\) for
    \(l \in \{n, m\}\). Then
    \begin{equation}
        |\varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}) - \varepsilon_m(\mathbf{k})|
        \geq |\varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}_0) - \varepsilon_m(\mathbf{k}_0)
        + L_n(\mathbf{k}) - L_m(\mathbf{k})|
        - |r_n(\mathbf{k})| - |r_m(\mathbf{k})|,
    \end{equation}
    by the reverse triangle inequality. Bounding the last two terms by
    \(Q_n(D)\) and \(Q_m(D)\) and taking the infimum over \(\mathbf{k} \in D\)
    and the minimum over \(m \neq n\) yields the claim.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Circular dependency.}
The quadratic remainders \(Q_n(D), Q_m(D)\) depend through \(\mu_{ij}\) on a positive
lower bound on the spectral gap. To break this circularity, the order-two gap
bound is built on top of the order-one and zero bounds: one first uses
~\eqref{eq:gap-order-1} to obtain a guaranteed lower bound on \(\Delta_n(D)\),
uses it to evaluate \(\mu_{ij}\) and \(Q_n(D)\), and then plugs the result
into~\eqref{eq:gap-order-2} to obtain a sharper estimate. The order-two gap
bound therefore \emph{refines} an existing certificate of non-degeneracy; it
cannot create one.
\begin{remark}
    Numerically, the bounds are obtained using interval arithmetic.
\end{remark}
\subsection{Bounds on eigenvalue derivatives}\label{sec:bounds-derivatives-nondegen}

To certify the applicability of the implicit function theorem in Saye's
algorithm, and to bound the integrand \(1/|\nabla \varepsilon_n|\)
of~\eqref{eq:dosgrad}, we need uniform bounds on \(\partial_i \varepsilon_n\)
on \(D\). They follow from~\autoref{lemma:second-deriv} and a first-order
Taylor expansion of \(\partial_i \varepsilon_n\) in \(\mathbf{k}\).

\begin{corollary}[First-order bound on eigenvalue derivatives]\label{cor:deriv-bound}
    Under the assumptions of~\autoref{lemma:second-deriv}, for all
    \(\mathbf{k} \in D\) and all \(i \in \{1,\dots,d\}\),
    \begin{equation}
        |\partial_i \varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}) - \partial_i
        \varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}_0)|
        \leq \sup_{\mathbf{q} \in D} \sum_{j=1}^d \mu_{ij}(\mathbf{q})\,
        \delta k_j,
    \end{equation}
    with \(\mu_{ij}\) defined in~\eqref{eq:mu-def}.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
    Apply Taylor-Lagrange at order \(1\) to \(\partial_i \varepsilon_n\) (which
    is \(\mathcal{C}^1\) on \(D\) under the non-degeneracy assumption): there
    exists \(\bm\xi \in [\mathbf{k}_0, \mathbf{k}] \subset D\) such that
    \begin{equation}
        \partial_i \varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}) - \partial_i \varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}_0)
        = \sum_{j=1}^d \partial_i \partial_j \varepsilon_n(\bm\xi)\,
        (k_j - k_{0,j}).
    \end{equation}
    Bounding each \(|\partial_i \partial_j \varepsilon_n(\bm\xi)|\) by
    \(\mu_{ij}(\bm\xi)\) via~\autoref{lemma:second-deriv} and using
    \(|k_j - k_{0,j}| \leq \delta k_j\) with \(\bm\xi \in D\) yields the claim.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
    The spectral gap \(\Delta_n(D)\) appears in \(\mu_{ij}\) and must be
    replaced by a computable lower bound from~\autoref{cor:gap-bounds} or
    \autoref{cor:gap-order-2}. The bound on \(|\nabla \varepsilon_n|\) is
    informative only on boxes where this lower bound is strictly positive.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Theoretical comparison of the bounds}\label{sec:bounds-comparison}

We summarise the bounds derived above and their hierarchy.
\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \begin{tabular}{c|c|c|c|c}
                                     & Sharpness                            &
        Non-degeneracy               & Easiness to compute                  &
        Numerically Computable
        \\
        \hline
        \eqref{lemma:weyl}           & \(\mathcal{O}(\delta \mathbf{k})\)   & No  & Uniform     & Yes \\

\section{Numerical evaluation of bounds}\label{sec:numerical-bounds}

The bounds of~\autoref{sec:bounds} are still expressed as suprema, over the
continuous box \(D\), of operator norms of \(H\) and its derivatives. To make
them effective, two operations must be replaced by computable proxies:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item the operator norm \(\|\cdot\|_\mathrm{op}\), which requires a spectral
          decomposition and does not propagate well through interval arithmetic;
    \item the supremum over \(\mathbf{q} \in D\), which is a continuous
          optimisation problem.
\end{enumerate}
We address each point in turn.

\subsection{From operator norm to Frobenius norm}\label{sec:frobenius-substitution}

The Frobenius norm \(\|A\|_F = (\sum_{ij} |A_{ij}|^2)^{1/2}\) is computed
directly from the matrix entries of \(A\) and is therefore propagated through
interval arithmetic by standard entrywise operations. The substitution rests
on the elementary inequality~\eqref{eq:op-vs-frob},
\begin{equation}
    \|A\|_\mathrm{op} \leq \|A\|_F \leq \sqrt{M}\,\|A\|_\mathrm{op},
\end{equation}
which trades a factor at most \(\sqrt{M}\) for direct entrywise computability.

Applying the substitution to the loose order-one
bound~\eqref{eq:eig-bound-loose} yields the practical bound
\begin{equation}
    |\varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}) - \varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}_0)|
    \leq \sup_{\mathbf{q} \in D}
    \sum_{j=1}^d \|\partial_j H(\mathbf{q})\|_F\, \delta k_j.
    \label{eq:eig-bound-frobenius}
\end{equation}
Likewise, the second-derivative bound~\eqref{eq:mu-def} becomes
\begin{equation}
    \mu_{ij}^F(\mathbf{q})
    := \|\partial_i \partial_j H(\mathbf{q})\|_F
    + \frac{2 \|\partial_i H(\mathbf{q})\|_F\,
        \|\partial_j H(\mathbf{q})\|_F}{\Delta_n(D)}
    \geq \mu_{ij}(\mathbf{q}),
\end{equation}
and the order-two bound~\eqref{eq:eig-bound-order-2} and derivative
bound~\autoref{cor:deriv-bound} hold verbatim with \(\mu_{ij}\) replaced by
\(\mu_{ij}^F\).

\begin{remark}[Why not Frobenius-substitute the tight
        bound~\eqref{eq:eig-bound-tight}?]\label{rem:tight-frobenius}
    Naively, one might attempt
    \begin{equation}
        \left\| \sum_j |\partial_j H(\mathbf{q})|\, \delta k_j
        \right\|_\mathrm{op}
        \leq \left\| \sum_j |\partial_j H(\mathbf{q})|\, \delta k_j
        \right\|_F
        \label{eq:tight-frobenius}
    \end{equation}
    however the absolute value of a hermitian matrix requires a diagonalisation, which is
    not stable under interval arithmetic. The bound~\eqref{eq:tight-frobenius} is
    therefore not computable, and we do not use it.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Frobenius pessimism in dimension \(M\)]
    The factor \(\sqrt{M}\) is conservative: it is attained only when all
    singular values are equal. For a tight-binding model with \(M\) Wannier
    bands and physically motivated sparsity (most matrix entries small), the
    actual ratio \(\|A\|_F / \|A\|_\mathrm{op}\) is typically much smaller than
    \(\sqrt{M}\).
\end{remark}
\subsection{Analytic bound from Fourier coefficients}\label{sec:bounds-analytic-fourier}

The Bloch transform~\eqref{eq:bloch-transform} expresses \(H\) as a finite
trigonometric polynomial in \(\mathbf{k}\). Differentiating term by term,
\begin{equation}
    \partial^{\bm\alpha} H(\mathbf{k})
    = \sum_{\mathbf{R} \in \mathcal{R}}
    (i\mathbf{R})^{\bm\alpha} \mathrm{e}^{i\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{R}}
    H_\mathbf{R},
\end{equation}
for any multi-index \(\bm\alpha = (\alpha_1, \dots, \alpha_d)\), with
\((\mathbf{R})^{\bm\alpha} := \prod_l R_l^{\alpha_l}\). The triangle inequality
on the Frobenius norm, applied entrywise after using \(|\mathrm{e}^{i\mathbf{k}
    \cdot \mathbf{R}}| = 1\), yields the global bound
\begin{equation}
    \sup_{\mathbf{k} \in \mathcal{B}} \|\partial^{\bm\alpha} H(\mathbf{k})\|_F
    \leq \sum_{\mathbf{R} \in \mathcal{R}}
    |\mathbf{R}^{\bm\alpha}|\, \|H_\mathbf{R}\|_F.
    \label{eq:fourier-bound}
\end{equation}
This bound is independent of the box \(D\), uniform on the entire Brillouin
zone, and \emph{precomputable} from the matrix entries of the
\((H_\mathbf{R})_\mathbf{R}\) at zero asymptotic cost. It directly furnishes
upper bounds on \(\|\partial_j H\|_F\) (taking \(\bm\alpha = \mathbf{e}_j\)) and
\(\|\partial_i \partial_j H\|_F\) (taking \(\bm\alpha = \mathbf{e}_i +
\mathbf{e}_j\)).

\begin{remark}[Pessimism of the Fourier bound]
    The bound~\eqref{eq:fourier-bound} discards all phase information from
    \(\mathrm{e}^{i\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{R}}\) and is therefore typically loose,
    by a factor that grows with the range of the \(H_\mathbf{R}\).
\end{remark}
\subsection{Interval arithmetic}\label{sec:interval-arithmetic}

The Frobenius substitution of~\autoref{sec:frobenius-substitution} reduces
every quantity entering our bounds to an entrywise expression in \(H\) and its
derivatives. Whenever such an expression must be evaluated as a supremum over a
box \(D\) — including the Frobenius substitute of
\(\sup_{\mathbf{q} \in D} \|H(\mathbf{q}) - H(\mathbf{k}_0)\|_\mathrm{op}\)
appearing in Weyl's inequality (\autoref{lemma:weyl}) and the derivative norms
\(\sup_{\mathbf{q} \in D} \|\partial^{\bm\alpha} H(\mathbf{q})\|_F\) — we
propagate guaranteed enclosures through the arithmetic by means of
\emph{interval arithmetic}~\cite{moore2009introduction}. We recall the elementary
rules in our setting.

\paragraph{Real intervals.}
A real interval is a closed connected subset \([a, b] \subset \mathbb{R}\).
Elementary operations are defined so that the result encloses every value
attainable from inputs in the operand intervals:
\begin{align}
    [a, b] + [c, d]     & = [a + c,\ b + d],                                         \\
    [a, b] - [c, d]     & = [a - d,\ b - c],                                         \\
    [a, b] \cdot [c, d] & = \bigl[\min(ac, ad, bc, bd),\ \max(ac, ad, bc, bd)\bigr].
\end{align}
Trigonometric functions \(\sin\) and \(\cos\) are evaluated by locating the
critical points (integer multiples of \(\pi/2\)) inside \([a, b]\): when none
lie inside, the bounds reduce to the values at the endpoints; otherwise they
are corrected to \(\pm 1\) accordingly.

\paragraph{Complex intervals.}
A complex interval is a Cartesian rectangle
\([a, b] + i\,[c, d] \subset \mathbb{C}\). Addition, subtraction, and
multiplication reduce to the real-interval rules applied to real and imaginary
parts. The only nontrivial operation in our setting is the unit-modulus phase
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{e}^{i [a, b]} = \cos[a, b] + i \sin[a, b].
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Application to the Bloch transform.}
For \(\mathbf{k} \in D\), each \(k_l\) is a real interval
\([k_{0, l} - \delta k_l,\ k_{0, l} + \delta k_l]\). For each
\(\mathbf{R} \in \mathcal{R}\), the dot product
\(\mathbf{k} \cdot \mathbf{R} = \sum_l k_l R_l\) is then a real interval and
\(\mathrm{e}^{i \mathbf{k} \cdot \mathbf{R}}\) is a complex interval. Term-by-term
differentiation of~\eqref{eq:bloch-transform} and summation give, for any
multi-index \(\bm\alpha\), an entrywise complex-interval matrix
\begin{equation}
    \bigl[\partial^{\bm\alpha} H(D)\bigr]_{pq}
    := \sum_{\mathbf{R} \in \mathcal{R}}
    (i\mathbf{R})^{\bm\alpha}\,
    \mathrm{e}^{i \mathbf{k} \cdot \mathbf{R}}\,
    [H_{\mathbf{R}}]_{pq},
    \label{eq:H-interval-enclosure}
\end{equation}
which by construction contains
\([\partial^{\bm\alpha} H(\mathbf{q})]_{pq}\) for every \(\mathbf{q} \in D\)
and every \(p, q\). The squared Frobenius norm is then bounded entrywise:
\begin{equation}
    \sup_{\mathbf{q} \in D} \|\partial^{\bm\alpha} H(\mathbf{q})\|_F^2
    \leq \sum_{p, q = 1}^M
    \max_{z \in [\partial^{\bm\alpha} H(D)]_{pq}} |z|^2,
    \label{eq:frobenius-interval}
\end{equation}
where each maximum is the squared distance from the origin to the farthest
corner of the complex rectangle, computable in closed form. The same
construction with \(H(\mathbf{q}) - H(\mathbf{k}_0)\) at entry level
(interval subtraction with the constant matrix \(H(\mathbf{k}_0)\)) gives
the Frobenius enclosure that makes Weyl's inequality effective on \(D\),
and~\eqref{eq:op-vs-frob} converts it back to an operator-norm bound at the
cost of an extra factor \(\sqrt{M}\).

\begin{remark}[Comparison with the analytic Fourier bound]
    The Fourier bound~\eqref{eq:fourier-bound}, obtained by the triangle
    inequality after using \(|\mathrm{e}^{i \mathbf{k} \cdot \mathbf{R}}| = 1\),
    is recovered from~\eqref{eq:H-interval-enclosure} when the phase
    \(\mathrm{e}^{i \mathbf{k} \cdot \mathbf{R}}\) is enclosed in the unit disc
    independently of \(D\). The full interval-arithmetic procedure retains the
    actual range of the phase on \(D\) and is therefore strictly tighter on
    small boxes, at the cost of one enclosure computation per \(D\). The
    Fourier bound, precomputable and box-independent, remains useful at the
    early stages of the subdivision where its lack of adaptivity is offset by
    its zero per-box cost.
\end{remark}



\subsubsection{Sampled bound with Lipschitz correction}\label{sec:bounds-sampling-sup}

An estimate can be obtained by sampling \(\Phi : \mathbf{q} \mapsto \|\partial_j
H(\mathbf{q})\|_F\) (or any other Frobenius-norm expression in \(H\) and its derivatives)
on a finite grid in \(D\) and adding a Lipschitz correction.

Let \(N \in \mathbb{N}^*\) and let \(D_N \subset D\) be a uniform discretized D with
\(N^d\) points such that any \(\mathbf{q}^* \in D\) lies within distance \(\delta k_j /
N\) of \(D_N\) along each coordinate \(j\).

\begin{theorem}[Guaranteed sampled bound]\label{thm:sampling}
    Let \(\Phi : D \to \mathbb{R}\) be continuously differentiable, and let
    \begin{equation}
        L_j(D) := \sup_{\mathbf{q} \in D} |\partial_j \Phi(\mathbf{q})|,
        \quad j \in \{1, \dots, d\}.
    \end{equation}
    Then
    \begin{equation}
        \sup_{\mathbf{q} \in D} \Phi(\mathbf{q})
        \leq \max_{\mathbf{q} \in D_N} \Phi(\mathbf{q})
        + \frac{1}{N} \sum_{j=1}^d L_j(D)\, \delta k_j.
        \label{eq:sampling-guaranteed}
    \end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
    Let \(\mathbf{q}^* \in D\) attain the supremum and \(\mathbf{q} \in D_N\)
    be such that \(|q_j^* - q_j| \leq \delta k_j / N\) for all \(j\) (such a
    \(\mathbf{q}\) exists by construction of \(D_N\)). The mean value theorem,
    applied along the segment from \(\mathbf{q}\) to \(\mathbf{q}^*\) (which
    lies in \(D\) by convexity), gives
    \begin{equation}
        |\Phi(\mathbf{q}^*) - \Phi(\mathbf{q})|
        \leq \sum_{j=1}^d \sup_{\mathbf{q}'' \in D}
        |\partial_j \Phi(\mathbf{q}'')|\, |q_j^* - q_j|
        \leq \frac{1}{N} \sum_{j=1}^d L_j(D)\, \delta k_j.
    \end{equation}
    Thus \(\Phi(\mathbf{q}^*) \leq \Phi(\mathbf{q})
    + \tfrac{1}{N} \sum_j L_j(D)\, \delta k_j
    \leq \max_{\mathbf{q}' \in D_N} \Phi(\mathbf{q}')
    + \tfrac{1}{N} \sum_j L_j(D)\, \delta k_j\), which
    is~\eqref{eq:sampling-guaranteed}.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Refined sampling versus subdivision.}
The bound~\eqref{eq:sampling-guaranteed} contains two contributions: the
sampled maximum, exact at the grid points, and a correction of order
\(\mathcal{O}(\|\bm{\delta\mathbf{k}}\|/N)\). As \(N \to \infty\) at fixed box,
the correction vanishes and the sampled bound converges to the true supremum.
A natural alternative to refining the sampling is to bisect \(D\) into
sub-boxes. Doubling \(N\) on \(D\) and halving each \(\delta k_j\) reduce the
correction by the same factor of \(2\); the two strategies are essentially

\section{Effective Hamiltonian and Pauli decomposition}
\label{sec:Heff}

When two eigenvalues coincide at a point \(\mathbf{k}_c\), the standard
Rayleigh--Schr\"odinger expansion becomes singular: the denominator
\(\varepsilon_{n,0}-\varepsilon_{m,0}\) vanishes for the crossing pair.  As a
consequence, the eigenvalues \(\varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k})\) generically lose
differentiability at \(\mathbf{k}_c\), even though \(H(\mathbf{k})\) remains smooth
(in fact analytic in the usual Bloch setting).

The standard remedy is to project onto the degenerate subspace and to diagonalise
the resulting low-dimensional Hamiltonian explicitly.  Let
\(\varepsilon_i(\mathbf{k}_c)=\varepsilon_j(\mathbf{k}_c)\) be a doubly degenerate eigenvalue
with associated orthonormal eigenvectors \(\psi_i(\mathbf{k}_c),\psi_j(\mathbf{k}_c)\),
and let
\begin{equation}
    P(\mathbf{k}_c)=\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_{\Gamma}\frac{\mathrm{i} z}{H(\mathbf{k}_c)-z},
    \label{eq:projector-contour}
\end{equation}
where \(\Gamma\) is a small positively oriented contour around the degeneracy.
Because of the analytic functional calculus, \(P(\mathbf{k}_c)\) is the orthogonal
projector onto the two-dimensional crossing subspace
\(\mathcal{E}_c=\operatorname{span}\{\psi_i,\psi_j\}\).

The \emph{effective Hamiltonian} near \(\mathbf{k}_c\) is the \(2\times2\) Hermitian
matrix
\begin{equation}
    H_{\mathrm{eff}}(\mathbf{k})
    = P(\mathbf{k}_c)\,H(\mathbf{k})\,P(\mathbf{k}_c),
    \label{eq:Heff}
\end{equation}
read in the fixed basis \((\psi_i,\psi_j)\).  Its eigenvalues coincide with the
two physical crossing eigenvalues of \(H(\mathbf{k})\) to leading order in
\(\mathbf{k}-\mathbf{k}_c\).  Throughout we work in a small box
\(D=\mathbf{k}_c+[-\delta k,\delta k]^d\) and we assume an \emph{exterior gap}
\(\gamma>0\): all other eigenvalues of \(H(\mathbf{k})\) stay at distance at least
\(\gamma\) from the crossing pair for every \(\mathbf{k}\in D\).

\subsection{Pauli decomposition}

Any \(2\times2\) Hermitian matrix admits a unique decomposition in the basis
\((I,\sigma_1,\sigma_2,\sigma_3)\), where the Pauli matrices are
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_1=\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix},
    \quad
    \sigma_2=\begin{pmatrix}0&\mathrm{i}\\-\mathrm{i}&0\end{pmatrix},
    \quad
    \sigma_3=\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&-1\end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
We write
\begin{equation}
    H_{\mathrm{eff}}(\mathbf{k})
    = a(\mathbf{k})\,I
    + \mathbf{b}(\mathbf{k})\cdot\bm{\sigma},
    \qquad a(\mathbf{k})\in\mathbb{R},
    \quad \mathbf{b}(\mathbf{k})\in\mathbb{R}^3.
    \label{eq:pauli-decomp}
\end{equation}
A direct computation gives the eigenvalues
\begin{equation}
    \lambda_{\pm}(\mathbf{k})
    = a(\mathbf{k})\pm\lVert\mathbf{b}(\mathbf{k})\rVert_2.
    \label{eq:pauli-eigenvalues}
\end{equation}
The two eigenvalues coincide if and only if \(\mathbf{b}(\mathbf{k})=\mathbf{0}\); this
is a system of three real equations in the \(d\)-imensional parameter
\(\mathbf{k}\).  For a generic Hamiltonian, crossings therefore occur on a set of
codimension \(3\): they are absent in \(d\le 2\) and form isolated points in \(d=3\)
(Weyl points).

In real materials, symmetries can constrain some components of \(\mathbf{b}\) to
vanish identically along high-symmetry submanifolds of the Brillouin zone,
lowering the effective codimension of the crossing condition.  Crossings of
codimension \(2\) (nodal lines) or codimension \(1\) (nodal surfaces) then become
possible and persist under any symmetry-preserving perturbation of \(H\).

\subsection{Linear expansion of the Pauli vector}

A first-order Taylor expansion of \(\mathbf{b}\) around the crossing point
\(\mathbf{k}_c\), where \(\mathbf{b}(\mathbf{k}_c)=\mathbf{0}\), yields
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{b}(\mathbf{k})
    =\sum_{i=1}^{d}\frac{\partial\mathbf{b}(\mathbf{k}_c)}{\partial k_i}\,
    \delta k_i
    +\mathcal{O}\!\bigl(\lVert\delta\mathbf{k}\rVert^2\bigr),
    \qquad \delta\mathbf{k}:=\mathbf{k}-\mathbf{k}_c.
\end{equation}
It is convenient to introduce the \(3\times d\) Jacobian matrix
\begin{equation}
    M_{i,j}:=\frac{\partial b_i}{\partial k_j}(\mathbf{k}_c),
    \qquad i=1,2,3,\quad j=1,\dots,d,
    \label{eq:Jacobi-M}
\end{equation}
so that, to leading order,
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{b}(\mathbf{k}) = M\,\delta\mathbf{k}
    +\mathcal{O}\!\bigl(\lVert\delta\mathbf{k}\rVert^2\bigr).
    \label{eq:b-linear}
\end{equation}

Combined with \eqref{eq:pauli-eigenvalues}, the local description of the two
crossing bands is
\begin{equation}
    \lambda_{\pm}(\mathbf{k})-a(\mathbf{k}_c)
    \approx \pm\Bigl\lVert\sum_{i=1}^{d}
    \frac{\partial\mathbf{b}(\mathbf{k}_c)}{\partial k_i}\,
    \delta k_i\Bigr\rVert_2
    \label{eq:local-cone}
\end{equation}
